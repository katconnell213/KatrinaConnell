My Teaching Philosophy

While I don't currently teach in a classroom, I find many ways to share the knowledge and skills I have acquired with others, and it is one of this things about my work that brings me the greatest joy. Nothing makes me happier than showing someone the one trick they needed to make something work or the time saving method to complete a large task in record time. I love sharing information and skills with people and seeing them take it and run with it. Below you will find a teaching philosophy statement for when I teach in a classroom setting, but my general teaching philosophy can be broken down in three simple components.

  1. Provide information in multiple formats:
    I prefer a combination of video and written educational materials that allow everyone a chance to consume the information at their own pace and to re-review as needed. I find that this helps everyone, not only the second-language English speakers that I originally wanted to aide with this strategy.
  2. Hands on is best
    Whether I'm in a classroom or the lab, I believe everyone understands concepts and procedures better when they do it themselves, so I always emphasize hands on activities.
  3. Scaffold learning and high-stakes assesments
    Learning procedures with expensive equipment can put people on edge and make them nervous. Though a combination of hands-on practice and scaffolding independence with "safety nets" such as calibrating an eye-tracker yourself, but with the lab manager in the room ready to help if needed, students are able to prove to themselves that they know what they are doing. Only then can they prove it to others. In a classroom, I adjust assignments to build skills iteratively to slowly prepare students for the culminative assesment, that builds confidence and allows for the inclusion of feedback which ultimatley increases learning.
